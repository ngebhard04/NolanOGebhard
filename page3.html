Congragulations on completing the hiring simulation

Even though this comes off as a normal, my simulation represent how algorithmic bias can emerge from decision rules and data patterns, reinforcing inequality by systematically favoring certain groups over others. The system demonstrate how seemingly natural algorithms can use indirect factors (such as names of background information) to produce biased hiring decisions, even when all relevant qualifications are the same.


Understanding Bias in the System

Even though it is hard to notice, there are many biased factors that could have influenced your outcome in the system.

Here is a list of what could give you a biased outcome based on how the system was coded:

  • Gender: The system penalizes female applicants in certain fields like technology, which can lead to unequal outcomes.
  • Age: Applicants over a certain age receive a lower score, even if they have the same qualifications.
  • Name: Certain names are used as a proxy for background or identity, which can introduce bias into the decision.
  • Job Type Matching: Some jobs are weighted differently, which may advantage certain groups over others.
  • Education Weighting: The system prioritizes certain education levels, which may reflect existing inequalities in access to education.

These factors demonstrate how algorithmic systems can produce biased outcomes, even when they appear to be neutral. The bias comes from the way the system was designed and the assumptions built into it.

Stakeholders in the System

The first stakeholder in the system would be the users trying to get an accurate result from the system. They are the ones that actually experience the consequence from the algorithm. Next would be the companies that use this system, as well as the developers of the system. The companies goals are to simplify the employment process and be able to limit their options quicker. The developers goals are to build an accurate system, while meeting the company requirements. Different stakeholders have different goals, such as fairness, efficiency, and accuracy, which can all lead to biased outcomes when one goal is prioritized over others. Overall, power and agency are unevenly distributed in the system. It leaves the developer and company in total control and leaving applicants with the least. The developers always have the most control, because they create the whole bias for the code being implimented into the system.

History of the System

Traditionally, hiring people has been done by humans, which let employers make decisions based on judgement. But this process has been proven to be slow, expensive, and prone to human bias. Companies began using automated systems to assist with hiring procedures in the early 2000s. By the 2010s, became even more advanced algorithmic hiring tools were being produced. As these systems became more common, researchers found that they could replicate and even amplify bias. A well known example is Amazons hiring algorithm, which was found to favor male candidates because it was trained on past hiring data that shows a male dominated workforce. This grew concerns about fairness, transparency, and accountability in automated hiring. My project creates a simplified algorithmic hiring system that demonstrates how bias can appear in automated decision making.

Manual or Automatic?

My system combines both manual input and automated decision making. The users enter their own information, and developers design the whole structure of the algorithm. But, the system processes inputs and outputs hiring decisions automatically. The most important decisions are automated, but the inputs are all from humans. My system primarily uses disciplinary architecture because it directly controls outcomes. The algorithm enforces decisions, it limits the users control and makes the decision for them. This system also has persuasive elements because users could change how they describe themselves and tweak some of their answers.

Infrastructure of the System

The infrastructure of the system is made up of both social and technical components that work together to produce hiring decisions. The technical elements are the algorithm or decision making model, the code that processes user input, and the dataset or rules that the system is based on. The social elements are the developers who design the system, the employers who decide to use it, and the applicants who interact with it. A breakdown in the system could occur if the data or rules used by the algorithm are biased or incomplete. If the system relies on biased training data, it may consistently favor one group over another.

Black Box in the System

In this system, the main black boxed component is the algorithm that produces hiring decisions. The processes being obscured are the way the algorithm weights different inputs, and how certain factors of the system are prioritized or penalized. The user inputs the provided information, and the outputs are the hiring decision and the score or ranking they receive. The developers and companies can understand the blackbox and the job applicants and the user of the system will not understand how their information is being processed.

Desired and Undesired Outcomes

The system was designed to make hiring more efficient by creating a system to process the information of users. The designer wants faster hiring decisions, reduced workload for employers, and more objective evaluation of candidates. However, existing classification systems such as gender, race, and socioeconomic background can shape how the system is built and how it functions. Undesired outcomes could be qualified candidates being unfairly rejected and certain groups being consistently disadvantaged. While the system aims to improve efficiency, it can unintentionally produce social biases embedded in its design and the data that was used.

Usefullness of a Sociotechnical Approach

A sociotechnical approach helps decide between the technical system and the social context it's used in. It shows that bias is not just a coding issue and it connects the algorithm to human decisions, social inequalities, and historical patterns. The system is shaped by both technology and society. The first solution would be to increase transparency and make the system more explainable to the users. I can show why a decision was made, or provide feedback on why they were rejected and so on. It will help users understand outcomes and make bias easier to detect and challenge. The second solution is to improve the dataset and design to reduce bias. I would have to use more training data, regularly audit the system for bias, and remove or adjust current biased patterns. This will reduce unequal outcomes across groups, and make the system more fair overall. By applying a sociotechnical approach, it becomes possible to identify both the technical and social sources of bias and develop solutions that make the system more fair, transparent, and accountable.
